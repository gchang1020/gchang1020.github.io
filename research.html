Research

My research spans geospatial data science, statistical modeling, and systems integration. Below are my current and completed projects, each with associated papers or manuscripts where applicable.

Research Interests

Authoritarian Politics & CCP Elite Studies

  • Elite recruitment, survival, and promotion: within the Chinese Communist Party: factional networks, geographic gatekeeping, and the personalization of rule under Xi Jinping
  • Comparative authoritarianism: regime durability, propaganda, cultural legitimation, and the strategic asymmetry between democracies and authoritarian states

Southeast Asia and Regional Influence

  • Dual dependence and foreign policy substitution: when economic pressure from China increases, do ASEAN states compensate by deepening security ties with the US, or do they capitulate? A time-series cross-sectional study of foreign policy responses across Southeast Asia
  • Elite capture vs. institutional resistance: in the Philippines, Cambodia, and Malaysia, Chinese economic penetration operates partly through elite-level deals; does the structure of domestic political institutions predict whether economic leverage translates into foreign policy concessions?

Japan, Korea, and Cross-Strait Tensions

  • Taiwan Strait signaling and CCP elite politics: do military provocations or diplomatic signals toward Taiwan correlate with internal factional dynamics or leadership cycles within the CCP?
  • Neighboring state perceptions of CCP factionalism: how do Japanese and Korean foreign policy establishments read and respond to internal shifts in Chinese elite composition?

Geopolitics and the Indopacific

  • Critical mineral diplomacy and regime type: does China preferentially secure agreements on lithium, cobalt, and nickel with autocratic or weakly institutionalized states, and does the US pattern differ?
  • AUKUS and Indopacific coalition durability: does AUKUS membership or the broader Indopacific framing shift Chinese military posture, BRI investment allocation, or diplomatic coercion? A before/after design around the 2021 announcement; a quantitative alignment measure (UN voting, arms sales, joint exercises, statement co-signing) to model what predicts coalition defection or drift toward China
  • India’s strategic hedging: modeling India’s Quad participation alongside its refusal to sanction Russia and its active border disputes with China, as a function of domestic politics and Chinese coercive pressure
  • Authoritarian diffusion and the CCP model: do countries with strong CCP-linked elite ties adopt similar nomenklatura-style elite selection mechanisms?

Methods & Analytical Strengths

  • Hierarchical and multilevel modeling: Bayesian hierarchical models (INLA), mixed-effects specifications (GLMM), and frailty models for survival analysis, with particular attention to nested and spatially structured random effects that identify place-based, institutional, and individual-level variation simultaneously
  • Geographic Information Systems: ArcGIS Pro, Google Earth Engine, spatial econometrics, remote sensing, suitability modeling, and the digitization and georeferencing of historical spatial data
  • Causal inference and time-series methods: difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, propensity score matching, and panel data designs applied to political and social science questions

Projects and Papers

Mitigating Flight Sequence Risk through Spatial-Temporal Modeling and Optimization

Top-7 Finalist Research Paper — American Airlines / UTD Challenge · 2026

Developed an end-to-end predictive and optimization framework to identify and minimize airline misconnection risk within hub-and-spoke networks using American Airlines operations at Dallas/Fort Worth (DFW). Constructed a large-scale spatial-temporal dataset of over 7 million inbound–outbound flight connection pairs from Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) records (2010–2019), enriched with operational, scheduling, weather, and network-congestion variables.

Implemented and compared multiple analytical frameworks including logistic regression, Bayesian hierarchical modeling (INLA), and XGBoost classification to estimate delay propagation risk. Achieved strong predictive performance (AUC = 0.988) while identifying key operational drivers such as inbound arrival delay, schedule tightness, destination congestion, and latent airport-level risk structures.

Integrated machine learning risk scores into a combinatorial optimization pipeline using Google OR-Tools integer programming to optimize connection assignments across the DFW hub network. The optimization reduced average predicted connection risk by 98% while avoiding over 78,000 high-risk itineraries, demonstrating the feasibility of proactive airline scheduling and network-risk mitigation using publicly available data.

Methods: Logistic Regression (GLM) · Bayesian Hierarchical (Spatial-Temporal ) Modeling with INLA · XGBoost Gradient Boosting · Feature Engineering · Optimization via Google OR-Tools Integer Programming · Greedy Heuristics · Predictive Analytics · Network Analysis

Data: U.S. Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) airline operational data (2010–2019), including arrival/departure records, weather delays, late-aircraft indicators, schedule gaps, and airport network features for American Airlines hub operations at Dallas/Fort Worth International Airport (DFW).
Geography of Power: Spatial Pathways to the CCP Central Committee

Dissertation Paper · 2026

Why do some places consistently produce more political elites than others? This paper examines the geographic origins of CCP Central Committee members across the 16th through 20th Party Congresses (2002–2022), using county-level administrative data for over 2,800 units across China. Combining generalized linear models with Bayesian spatiotemporal models estimated via Integrated Nested Laplace Approximation (INLA), the study tests whether elite production reflects local spatial spillovers, structural accessibility, or historically embedded institutional advantage.

The results show that elite production is highly concentrated and deeply uneven, the median county produces zero Central Committee members across the entire twenty-year period, while Beijing and Shanghai dominate. Critically, this concentration does not arise from geographic neighborhood diffusion: adjacency-based spatial models (BESAG/BYM2) contribute negligible explanatory power over non-spatial alternatives, meaning elite advantages do not spill across borders to neighboring counties. Instead, geographic effects operate through proximity to national metropolitan centers and historical railway connectivity dating to the 1960s, the formative years of the current elite cohort, consistent with theories of political socialization and center-periphery state penetration.

The most striking finding is temporal: an autoregressive spatiotemporal specification reveals that geographic advantage persists almost perfectly across successive Party Congresses, with an autoregressive parameter ρ ≈ 0.997. Localities that produced elites in 2002 are, to a remarkable degree, the same localities producing elites in 2022. This is not a product of observable structural characteristics, location-level random effects that capture unobserved institutional embeddedness account for most of the explanatory gain over the baseline model. The findings reframe elite circulation as a spatially and temporally embedded process, in which access to national power is structured by durable place-based hierarchies rather than factional or organizational mechanisms alone.

Methods: GLM (Gaussian log-log) · Bayesian INLA · CAR/BYM2 spatial models · AR1 spatiotemporal specification · Web scraping + LLM-assisted data preprocessing · ArcGIS Pro (historical railway digitization) · Google Earth Engine

Data: CCP Central Committee biographies (16th–20th Congresses) · County-level administrative boundaries (ESRI, GADM, MCA) · 1960s railway network (manually digitized) · Digital Elevation Model (OpenTopography) · County population (ESRI 2021)
Who Survives? Elite Retention and the Mechanisms of Durability in the CCP Central Committee

Dissertation Paper · 2026

While most research on Chinese elite politics asks who enters or rises within the CCP, this paper asks a distinct question: once officials reach the Central Committee, who stays? Drawing on a comprehensive panel of individual career histories across the 16th through 20th Party Congresses, the study examines the institutional, relational, and demographic mechanisms that determine whether a Central Committee member survives into the next congressional session.

The paper contributes to three debates. On authoritarian institutionalization: age-based retirement norms retain binding force even under Xi Jinping’s consolidation, while relational embeddedness continues to structure intra-elite resilience within formally bounded career trajectories. On institutional versus relational accounts of elite politics: static factional labels are weaker predictors of survival than dynamic network embeddedness — CYL affiliation carries no independent predictive power once age, career background, and network ties are controlled — pointing toward relational rather than factional interpretations of post-entry durability. On temporal stability: the relative weight of credentials versus networks shifts across leadership periods, with formal qualifications providing more reliable protection during consolidation as inherited patronage structures are disrupted.

Three estimators are combined for robustness: logistic regression models estimate the baseline probability of survival between congresses; Cox proportional hazards models incorporate the timing of exit with individual-level frailty terms and congress-stratified baseline hazards; and mixed-effects logistic models account for repeated observations within individuals and unobserved geographic clustering through birthplace-based random intercepts. The 18th Party Congress marks a sharp increase in elite exit relative to earlier periods, consistent with heightened reshuffling during Xi’s consolidation.

Methods: Logistic regression (GLM) · Cox proportional hazards (frailty + stratified baseline) · Mixed-effects logistic models (GLMM) · LLM-assisted biographical data preprocessing · Relational embeddedness index construction

Data: CCP Central Committee biographies (16th–20th Congresses) · Individual career histories, factional affiliations, educational credentials, network ties
Who Advances? Elite Selection and the Transformation of CCP Promotion Logic

Dissertation Paper · 2026

This paper investigates what determines promotion from the Central Committee to the Politburo, with particular attention to whether the criteria governing that transition changed after the 18th Party Congress. The central argument is that institutionalized factional balance, which structured elite advancement during the Hu Jintao era, has been progressively displaced by leader-centered selection under Xi Jinping, with Communist Youth League (CYL) network affiliation transforming from an asset into a liability.

Using a panel of 281 eligible elite observations across the 16th through 20th Congresses, the paper combines descriptive statistics, network visualization, and logistic regression (GLM and GLMM) to evaluate promotion patterns. CYL Central Committee background is treated as an observable proxy for the Tuanpai faction, whose political value can be tracked before and after the regime shift. The results are clear: prior to the 18th Congress, a CYL-affiliated official at the sample mean had an estimated Politburo promotion probability of approximately 42%, versus 8% for an otherwise identical non-CYL official. After the 18th Congress that advantage reverses, CYL-affiliated officials drop to roughly 9% while non-CYL officials rise to 17%. The interaction between CYL affiliation and the post-18th period is consistently negative and significant across all model specifications, and the penalty is factional rather than generational: it is concentrated specifically in officials with CYL backgrounds, not in the post-18th cohort broadly. Network visualizations of Politburo co-membership show a visible cluster of CYL-linked elites in the 17th and 18th Congress cohorts that disappears almost entirely by the 19th and is fully absent by the 20th. These findings suggest a hybrid rather than wholesale transformation of elite selection: technocratic credentials retain modest positive associations with promotion, but factional networks that once provided institutional protection have been selectively dismantled.

Methods: Logistic regression (GLM) · Mixed-effects logistic regression (GLMM) · Network visualization · Congress fixed effects · LLM-assisted biographical data preprocessing

Data: CCP Central Committee and Politburo membership (16th–20th Congresses) · Individual career histories, CYL affiliation, educational credentials
Bayesian Spatiotemporal Modeling of Renal Disease Mortality Across Texas Counties, 2010–2019

Master’s Thesis Paper · 2025

Where in Texas do people die of kidney disease at elevated rates, and why do those places persist as high-risk even after accounting for income, education, race, and access to care? This paper examines county-level chronic kidney disease mortality across all 254 Texas counties over a ten-year panel, using a suite of eight competing Bayesian hierarchical models to evaluate how different spatial and temporal specifications shape inference about mortality disparities. The central methodological question, which combination of spatial prior and temporal structure best represents underlying patterns, drives a systematic comparison that has not previously been applied to county-level ESRD mortality in Texas.

The modeling framework compares specifications along two dimensions: spatial structure, ranging from independent random effects (IID) through proper conditional autoregressive priors (Besagproper2) to the Besag–York–Mollié formulation (BYM2), and temporal structure, from no temporal component through IID and first-order random walk (RW1) to county-specific random slopes. The preferred model, sBesagproper2_tRW1_cov, was selected over the lower-DIC but more heavily parameterized BYM2 specification on grounds of parsimony, interpretability, and resistance to overfitting given the ten-year temporal window. Model adequacy was assessed via DIC and residual Moran’s I, which drops to statistically insignificant levels only once spatial random effects are included, confirming that non-spatial specifications leave meaningful autocorrelation in their residuals.

Fixed-effect estimates from the preferred model show that higher median household income and educational attainment are associated with reduced mortality risk, while disability prevalence and, in the more complex BYM2 specification, larger African American and American Indian/Alaska Native population shares are associated with elevated risk. Spatially, excess mortality persists in South Texas (Jim Hogg, Dimmit, Brooks, Maverick, Duval, Jim Wells) and in isolated pockets of West and Northeast Texas even after covariate adjustment, implying unmeasured contextual drivers, environmental exposures, healthcare infrastructure deficits, or cultural barriers, that structured spatial priors absorb but observable variables cannot explain. Temporally, statewide relative risk rose steadily from 2010 through 2018 before declining sharply in 2019, a trajectory consistent with gradual widening of population-level disparities followed by a possible shift in reporting or treatment access near the decade’s end.

The most important methodological contribution is demonstrating that model specification choices are not neutral: a baseline Poisson GLM with no spatial or temporal structure yields a residual Moran’s I of 0.061 (p = 0.045) and a proportion-based R² of just 0.014, while the preferred spatiotemporal specification achieves R² of 0.421 and residually white-noise errors. The findings establish that renal mortality in Texas is shaped by durable place-based disadvantage that standard regression cannot capture, and that Bayesian INLA modeling offers a computationally tractable, principled framework for making those place-based structures visible and policy-actionable.

Methods: Bayesian hierarchical modeling via INLA · Besagproper2 / BYM2 / IID spatial priors · RW1 / AR1 temporal specifications · DIC model comparison · Global Moran’s I residual diagnostics · Proportion-based R² · McFadden pseudo-R² · Poisson log-rate regression with population offset · VIF multicollinearity pruning · ArcGIS (minimum distance computation) · R-INLA

Data: USRDS county-level renal mortality counts (2010–2019) · U.S. Census population denominators · NHGIS 5-Year ACS socioeconomic covariates · U.S. Census decennial data (2010, 2020) · Dialysis facility accessibility scores (Kim et al., 2024, two-stage floating catchment method) · Texas county shapefile (CNTY_FIPS)
The Price of Being Right: Why Moral Superiority Can’t Win Global Power

Working Paper, 2025

Liberal democracies have long mistaken moral restraint for strategic strength. This paper argues that forty years of engagement policy empowered authoritarian competitors by granting them trade access, capital, and technology while expecting political liberalization in return, a miscalculation that produced strategic dependency rather than convergence. Drawing on realist IR theory and comparative politics, the paper identifies a fundamental asymmetry: democracies are norm-bound and procedurally constrained while authoritarian rivals are result-oriented and unconstrained. The paper calls for democracies to rediscover strategic realism, reclaiming industrial capacity, forging alliances of production, and developing bounded mechanisms of collective mobilization, without abandoning the liberal principles that define them.

Keywords: strategic asymmetry · liberal order · authoritarian competition · democratic resilience · strategic realism DOI: 10.2139/ssrn.5667512
Autocratic Survival Beyond Institutions: History, Culture, and Propaganda

Working Paper, 2025

Why do some authoritarian regimes endure for decades while others collapse? This paper critically engages Gandhi and Przeworski’s influential institutionalist account, which holds that legislatures and parties stabilize dictatorships by structuring elite bargains, and argues that formal institutions cannot explain durability on their own. Drawing on case studies of China, the Soviet Union, and North Korea alongside scholarship by Perry, Fitzpatrick, and Wedeen, the paper shows that authoritarian resilience depends on three additional pillars: revolutionary historical narratives that generate legitimacy, cultural traditions of hierarchy and obedience (particularly Confucian filial piety in the Chinese case), and propaganda systems that function not as mere messaging but as institutionalized discipline saturating everyday life. A synthetic framework integrating all four dimensions is proposed.

Keywords: authoritarianism · autocratic survival · institutions · propaganda · Confucianism · China · USSR · North Korea DOI: 10.2139/ssrn.5523118
Detecting Objects in Drone Photos with YOLO, Roboflow, and Label-Studio

Term Research Paper · 2025

How can a practitioner systematically manage the messy, multi-step workflow of a drone-based object detection project without losing track of files, formats, and experimental parameters across platforms? This project develops PyYolo, a Python CLI tool designed to integrate labeling platforms (Roboflow, Label-Studio), model training, and inference into a single, reproducible pipeline, then applies it to detecting pre-fabricated tube structures at a construction site from aerial drone imagery.

The core challenge is organizational as much as technical. A typical YOLO-based detection project spans image splitting, platform-specific annotation, format conversion, model training across multiple configurations, and threshold-tuned inference, each step generating its own file artifacts in incompatible structures. PyYolo addresses this by standardizing directory trees, serializing run configurations into folder names (e.g., s-20-640-2_pred_0.25 encodes model size, epoch, image size, batch, and confidence threshold), and converting exports from both Roboflow and Label-Studio, including COCO JSON format, into the YOLOv8 dataset specification.

The system was validated on aerial imagery from the OpenDroneMap Caliterra dataset, targeting pre-fab tube objects scattered across a construction site. Using a Small YOLOv8 model trained over 20 epochs, the pipeline detected 31 tubes in a test image at a confidence threshold of 0.25, with confidence scores ranging from 0.251 to 0.481. The F1-confidence curve peaked at 0.78 near a threshold of 0.291, reflecting a model with strong recall at low thresholds but rapidly degrading precision above 0.8, a pattern consistent with the small apparent size of objects in overhead imagery and their spatially clustered distribution within the frame. Perfect precision (1.00) was achievable only at confidence = 0.865, at the cost of significant missed detections.

The project reframes drone-based object detection as a data management problem as much as a modeling one. The value of PyYolo lies not in any single detection result, but in making experiments reproducible, comparable, and extensible, enabling systematic sweeps over model sizes, training parameters, and confidence thresholds that would otherwise require manual bookkeeping across platforms.

Methods: YOLOv8 (Nano / Small / Medium) · Python CLI (PyYolo) · Label-Studio (YOLO + COCO export) · Roboflow (YOLO export) · Format conversion pipeline · F1 / Precision / Recall curve analysis

Data: OpenDroneMap Caliterra aerial drone imagery · Label-Studio annotations (bounding box, tube class) · YOLOv8 training/validation splits · Inference results across confidence thresholds (0.01 – 0.25)
Damage Assessment of Wenchang Space Center after Typhoon Yagi

Term Research Project · 2024

How much did Typhoon Yagi damage China’s Wenchang Space Launch Site? This project uses pre- and post-typhoon satellite imagery to detect and quantify vegetation loss at the facility following Yagi’s landfall on Hainan Island around August 30, 2024, one of the strongest typhoons to hit the island in recent memory, with peak winds of 145 mph and a death toll of 46.

Sentinel-2 L2A imagery (10–20m resolution, 9 spectral bands) was sourced from the European Copernicus data center and Google Earth Engine, with two acquisitions bracketing the storm: August 4 (pre-typhoon) and September 13 (post-typhoon). The study area was defined by a bounding box over the space center on the northeastern coast of Hainan. All image processing was performed in Python rather than ArcGIS Pro, to develop a deeper, parameter-level understanding of each analytical step.

The core damage assessment compares NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) between the two dates. Without atmospheric correction, the analysis estimates 63.2% of the site as damaged; after applying a CLAHE-based dehazing procedure, which reduces blue-band scattering that inflates NIR-to-red ratios, the estimate drops to 50.6%, illustrating how haze can systematically overstate apparent vegetation loss. Additional processing steps included image denoising, histogram equalization for visual enhancement, and Principal Component Analysis for haze characterization via NDHI (Normalized Difference Haze Index). The NDHI histograms confirmed a measurable post-storm shift toward lower haze values, consistent with the changed atmospheric and surface conditions after the typhoon passed.

The project demonstrates that open-access satellite data combined with Python-based remote sensing workflows can provide meaningful rapid damage assessments for sensitive infrastructure, while also underscoring the importance of atmospheric correction in change-detection analyses.

Methods: Sentinel-2 L2A band compositing · NDVI & NDHI spectral indices · Histogram equalization · CLAHE dehazing (LAB color space) · Image denoising · PCA (pseudo-band decomposition) · Raster change detection with threshold classification

Data: Sentinel-2 L2A (Copernicus / Google Earth Engine) · Study area: Wenchang Space Launch Site, Hainan, China (110.94°E–110.96°E, 19.61°N–19.62°N)
Finding Preferable Locations to Build Hybrid Renewable Energy Systems in Tanzania

Term Research Project · 2023

Where in Tanzania should hybrid solar-wind energy plants be built? This project applies GIS-based Suitability Modeling to identify viable sites for hybrid renewable energy systems in Tanzania, a country where over 85% of the population still relies on traditional fuels and rural electrification remains below 17%. Because solar and wind resources are complementary by nature (solar peaks during the day, wind at night), hybrid systems offer more stable output than either source alone and lower total investment costs, making them especially well-suited to Tanzania’s energy access challenges.

The analysis follows the weighted overlap workflow in ArcGIS Pro’s Suitability Modeler: defining criteria, sourcing and cleaning multi-format raster and vector data from 15+ providers (including DIVA-GIS, Global Solar Atlas, Global Wind Atlas, WorldPop, and Protected Planet), reprojecting and converting all layers to a consistent coordinate system, and transforming each into a 0–1 preference surface using appropriate functions (Gaussian for wind and solar thresholds, Large for distance buffers, SymmetricLinear for aspect). Ten criteria governed site selection: minimum wind speed of 5–6 m/s and solar radiation of 4.5 kWh/m²/day, slope under 3%, aspect within ±60°, and required setbacks from airports (3,000m), residential areas (2,000m), water bodies (400m), protected areas (500m), roads and railroads (500m), and dense population zones. Aspect carried the highest weight (29.85%), followed by wind and solar at 18.91% each.

Two output scenarios were produced: one identifying 25 candidate sites and one selecting the 3 optimal locations. Both scenarios converge on the same two clusters, one in the north-central interior (approximately 36.70°E, 5.34°S) and one in the south-central region (approximately 37.48°E, 10.32°S), areas that score highly on solar and wind potential while satisfying all exclusion criteria. The study notes that results could be further refined with data on cultivated land, existing transmission infrastructure, and a more theoretically grounded population density threshold.

Methods: Weighted Suitability Modeling · ArcGIS Pro Suitability Modeler · Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) · Raster transformation (Gaussian, Large, Small, Near, SymmetricLinear functions) · Manual shoreline digitization · Coordinate reprojection (GCS WGS 1984 → WGS 1984 Web Mercator)

Data: Global Solar Atlas · Global Wind Atlas · DIVA-GIS (elevation, land cover, population, roads, inland water) · WorldPop 2020 · Protected Planet (WDPA) · ArcGIS Living Atlas · OurAirports · RCMRD Africa GeoPortal (forest and game reserves) · NGDC/NOAA coastlines
